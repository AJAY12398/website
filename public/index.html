Control your Content

Online content authoring isn't a solved problem. You might go with an HTML-based editor, and hope it gives you the kind of HTML you want. Or you might go with a text-based markup format, and hope your users understand how to use it.

ProseMirror aims to improve that situation by providing a drop-in editor component with a rigid semantic document model, that can be customized to fit your application. By default, it exposes the CommonMark (Markdown) document model, but you can restrict that and/or add your own kinds of elements.

By strictly separating its document data structure from the browser's DOM, ProseMirror sidesteps most of the mess that is contentEditable, and ensures that the document stays entirely under the control of the editor.

Collaborate

ProseMirror represents changes to documents as well-defined pieces of data. Such changes can be stored, inverted (for undo), analyzed (think git blame), and filtered (to implement more complex types of content restriction).

And most importantly, they can be sent over the wire to implement real-time collaborative editing. The library solves the hard problems (see this writeup), and you can simply wire it up to your transport of choice (think long-polling or websockets). You can see this in action in our demo.

Integrate and Extend

ProseMirror is implemented as a set of clean ES6 modules. I was able to apply much of my editor-implementing experience (as the author of CodeMirror) to make this editor fast, robust, and open to extension.

The codebase was written with extendability in mind. ProseMirror adjusts to your site, not the other way around. External code can extend the document model, attach event handlers, register new options, trigger editing actions, and so on.

Status

ProseMirror is intended to become open source (MIT licenced), but since I need to combine writing open software and earning a living, I am running a crowd-funding campaign to fund the work I put into the implementation. Until that succeeds, all rights are reserved.

The library is in beta status. It works, as the demos can attest, but there are without doubt a lot of wrinkles left that need to be ironed out with real-world testing.
